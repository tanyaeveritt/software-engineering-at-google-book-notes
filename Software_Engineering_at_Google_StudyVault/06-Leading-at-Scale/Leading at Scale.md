## The Three "Always" of Leadership
As a leader scales from one team to many teams, effectiveness depends on three principles: **Always Be Deciding**, **Always Be Leaving**, and **Always Be Scaling**. The leader's role shifts from technical depth to broad strategic intuition.
- Prior engineering expertise becomes less relevant; **general technical intuition** matters more
- Leading teams of teams is more about **organizing people** than being a technical wizard
- The process is demoralizing at first but yields much greater impact over time

## Always Be Deciding
Leaders at scale make high-level decisions about **trade-offs**, not specific engineering tasks. The process has three steps: identify the **blinders** (assumptions people make without realizing), identify the **key trade-offs**, and then **decide and iterate**.
- Important problems are **ambiguous** — no magic "silver bullet" solutions
- Decisions are best for "this particular month" — rebalance next month
- Avoid **analysis paralysis** by framing decisions as experiments: "We'll try this and revisit"
- **Case Study**: Google Web Search balanced Quality, Latency, and Capacity — "Good, Fast, Cheap — pick two"

## Always Be Leaving
The goal is to build an organization that **solves problems without you**. "Always Be Leaving" (coined by Bharat Mediratta) means you should not be a **Single Point of Failure (SPOF)**.
- **Litmus test**: If you disappear for a week, does the team keep succeeding?
- Build a **self-driving team** through three steps: divide the problem space, delegate subproblems to leaders, and iterate
- **Delegation** is the primary mechanism for training leaders — let them fail, retry, and learn
- Ask daily: "What can I do that **nobody else** on my team can do?"
- Anchor team identity to a **problem**, not a product/solution — products can be replaced; problems are evergreen

## Always Be Scaling
Success generates more responsibility. You must proactively manage scaling to protect your **time, attention, and energy**.
- The **Cycle of Success** is a spiral: Analysis → Struggle → Traction → Reward (new problem!) → **Compression** (do prior work with half the people)
- **Important vs Urgent** (Eisenhower): urgent things are rarely important; important things are rarely urgent
- Three techniques: **Delegate** urgent tasks, **schedule dedicated time** for strategic work, **find a tracking system** that works (e.g., Getting Things Done)

## Learn to Drop Balls
Apply the **Marie Kondo** principle to your task list: identify the top **20%** of critical tasks only you can do, and deliberately drop the other 80%.
- Sub-leaders will often pick up the middle 60% automatically
- Truly critical items will migrate back up to you if ignored
- This frees you to focus on high-level strategy

## Protecting Your Energy
Managing energy is as important as managing time.
- **Take real vacations** (at least a week, fully disconnected)
- **Make it trivial to disconnect** (e.g., work profile on phone)
- **Take real weekends** — sign out Friday, sign in Monday
- **Take breaks** during the day (brain operates in 90-minute cycles)
- **Mental health days** — your bad mood sets the tone for everyone; go home if needed
