## The Genius Myth
The **Genius Myth** is the tendency to ascribe the success of a team to a single person/leader.
- Linus Torvalds wrote a proof-of-concept kernel; Linux was built by **thousands** of smart people
- Michael Jordan's "true genius was in the way he worked with his team" under Phil Jackson's coaching
- Deep down, many engineers secretly wish to be seen as geniuses — this fantasy is counterproductive
- "You are probably not a genius" — and even if you are, genius alone is insufficient without social skills
- 100% of work at Google requires a **minimal level of social skills**

## Hiding Considered Harmful
Working alone is inherently **riskier** than working with others.
- **Early Detection**: sharing early catches fundamental design mistakes and prevents reinventing wheels
- **Bus Factor**: if only one person understands the code, the project is toast if they leave
- **Pace of Progress**: tight feedback loops (like compiling frequently) apply to whole projects too
- "Many eyes make sure your project stays **relevant and on track**"
- Ideal setup: groups of **4-8 people** in small rooms for spontaneous conversation
- "Fail early, fail fast, fail often" — get feedback as early as possible

## The Bus Factor
> **Bus factor** (noun): the number of people that need to get hit by a bus before your project is completely doomed.

- Ensure at least good **documentation** plus a primary and secondary owner for each area
- "It is better to be one part of a successful project than the critical part of a failed project"
- People don't literally get hit by buses, but they do get married, move away, leave the company, or take leave

## Three Pillars of Social Interaction (HRT)
The foundation on which all healthy interaction and collaboration are based:
- **Pillar 1: Humility** — You are not the center of the universe. You're open to self-improvement.
- **Pillar 2: Respect** — You genuinely care about others. You treat them kindly and appreciate their abilities.
- **Pillar 3: Trust** — You believe others are competent and will do the right thing. You let them drive when appropriate.
- Root-cause analysis of almost any social conflict traces back to a lack of humility, respect, and/or trust

## HRT in Practice
Concrete behaviors that embody humility, respect, and trust:
- **Lose the ego**: build collective team ego, not personal ego. "The appearance of conforming gets you a long way" (Hamming)
- **Give and take criticism**: "you are not your code" — constructive criticism is about the code, not the person. Say "I'm confused by..." not "You got it wrong"
- **Fail fast and iterate**: "Failure is an option" at Google. Google X rewards disproving ideas. Thomas Edison: "If I find 10,000 ways something won't work, I haven't failed"
- **Blameless postmortems**: document what was learned and what changes; include summary, timeline, cause, impact, action items, and lessons learned
- **Learn patience**: different working styles (top-down vs bottom-up) can coexist with trust and patience
- **Be open to influence**: "the more open you are to influence, the more you are able to influence." Admitting "I don't know" increases long-term credibility

## Blameless Post-Mortem Culture
A proper postmortem should always contain:
- A brief **summary** of the event
- A **timeline** from discovery through investigation to resolution
- The **primary cause** of the event
- **Impact and damage** assessment
- Action items to **fix immediately** (with owners)
- Action items to **prevent recurrence**
- **Lessons learned**
- Must NOT be a list of apologies, excuses, or finger-pointing

## Being Googley
Google defined explicit attributes for "Googleyness" to replace the vague, bias-prone informal test:
- **Thrives in ambiguity**: can build consensus and progress even when the environment shifts
- **Values feedback**: humble enough to receive and give feedback gracefully
- **Challenges status quo**: sets ambitious goals despite resistance or inertia
- **Puts the user first**: empathy and respect for users; pursues actions in their best interests
- **Cares about the team**: actively helps coworkers without being asked
- **Does the right thing**: strong ethics; willing to make difficult or inconvenient decisions
