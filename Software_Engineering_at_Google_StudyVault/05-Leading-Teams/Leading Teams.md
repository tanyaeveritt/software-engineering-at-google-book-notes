## Three Leadership Roles
Google recognizes distinct leadership roles with different skill requirements:
- **Engineering Manager**: responsible for performance, productivity, and **happiness** of every person on the team; balances business needs with individual needs
- **Tech Lead (TL)**: responsible for technical aspects -- technology decisions, architecture, priorities, velocity, project management; usually also an individual contributor
- **Tech Lead Manager (TLM)**: handles both people and technical needs; common on small/nascent teams; requires high degree of mentoring from experienced TLMs
- Larger teams at Google typically have a **pair**: one TL and one engineering manager working as partners

## Influence Without Authority
One of the most powerful leadership traits you can develop.
- Jeff Dean led only a fraction of Google's engineering but influenced technical decisions across the entire org
- **Data Liberation Front**: fewer than 6 engineers got 50+ Google products to export data via Google Takeout -- no executive directive, just identifying strategic need and making integration easy
- Key: identify strategic need, link to company mission, and build tools that make it easy for others to participate

## Moving from IC to Leadership
Leadership often happens **accidentally** ("manageritis") -- someone starts resolving conflicts and coordinating people.
- **Reasons people avoid management**: less time writing code, intangible output ("I didn't pick any apples today" -- but banana trees are flourishing), Peter Principle fear, exposure only to bad managers
- **Reasons to consider it**: a way to **scale yourself**, you might be good at it, someone has to lead
- Google requires performing above current level before promotion -- mitigates Peter Principle
- "Resist the urge to manage" -- Steve Vinter's best advice for new managers

## Servant Leadership
The cure for the "management disease" -- serve your team like a butler tends to a household.
- Create an atmosphere of **humility, respect, and trust**
- Remove bureaucratic obstacles, help achieve consensus, buy dinner when team works late
- Manage both **technical and social** health of the team
- Fill in the cracks and advise when necessary, but get hands dirty too
- "The only managing that a servant leader does is to manage both the technical and social health of the team"

## Failure Is an Option
Building psychological safety so teams can take greater risks.
- "If you try to achieve an impossible goal, there's a good chance you'll fail, but you'll accomplish far more than if you attempted something you knew you could complete"
- **Fail fast** = good (less at stake); fail slowly = painful (more at risk); fail affecting customers = worst but most structured learning
- Google performs **postmortems** on every major production failure -- focuses on core problem, not finger-pointing
- Praise individual successes **publicly**; give constructive criticism **privately**

## Antipatterns (What NOT to Do)

### Hire Pushovers
Hiring people less smart or ambitious cements your position but means **more work** for you and zero team autonomy. Instead, hire people **smarter than you** who can replace you.

### Ignore Low Performers
"Hope is not a strategy" (Google SRE motto). Ignoring low performers causes high performers to leave and team morale to leak. Coach low performers with **specific time frames, small measurable goals**, and weekly check-ins. Help them up or out.

### Ignore Human Issues
Managers promoted from technical roles often neglect the **human element**. Example: manager Pablo forced new father Jake to come to the office despite unchanged productivity. A little empathy goes a long way.

### Be Everyone's Friend
Don't confuse friendship with leading with a soft touch. When you hold power over someone's career, they may feel pressure to **artificially reciprocate** friendship. Have lunch together, but maintain appropriate boundaries.

### Compromise the Hiring Bar
"A people hire other A people; B people hire C people" (Steve Jobs). Never pick the best 5 from 50 regardless of whether they meet the bar. The cost of a bad hire (lost productivity, stress, firing) far exceeds the cost of finding the right person.

### Treat Team Like Children
People act the way you treat them. If you treat them like children, they'll behave like children. Trust employees and they'll rise to the occasion. Google example: **unlocked office supply cabinets and self-service Tech Stops** -- employees are trusted to do the right thing.

## Positive Patterns

### Lose the Ego
Cultivate a strong **collective** team ego, not personal ego. Trust your team -- they know the details better than you. Appreciate inquiry. **Apologize when you make a mistake** -- it gains respect, not vulnerability.

### Be a Zen Master
Mediate your reactions. The leader is **always on stage** -- your visible attitude is unconsciously noticed and spreads infectiously. Bill Coughran never panicked, which calmed panicked engineers. **Ask questions** instead of jumping to solution mode -- help people solve problems themselves (Socratic method).

### Be a Catalyst
Bring reactants into close proximity. **Build consensus** -- this is how you lead without authority. Even voluntary concession of authority to team leads is a form of consensus.

### Remove Roadblocks
Jump in to unblock the team. You don't need to know all the answers -- **knowing the right person** is often more valuable than knowing the right answer.

### Be a Teacher and Mentor
Watching a junior member spend 3 hours on what you could do in 20 minutes is painful but vital. Balance a mentee's time learning vs contributing. Three things needed: experience, ability to explain, and ability to gauge how much help is needed.

### Set Clear Goals
Create a **concise mission statement**. Without clear goals, each team member pulls the product in a slightly different direction, wasting energy. Clear priorities help the team make trade-offs.

### Be Honest
"I won't lie to you, but I will tell you when I can't tell you something or if I just don't know." Avoid the **compliment sandwich** -- most people only hear the compliments. Be kind and empathetic but deliver clear, direct feedback. The "train" metaphor: pick which trains to stop -- don't jump in front of every one.

### Track Happiness
Ask "What do you need?" at the end of every one-on-one. Track careers -- most people want to be promoted, learn something new, launch something important, and work with smart people. Rate happiness 1-10. Spread grungy tasks evenly. Be sensitive to personal situations.

## People Are Like Plants
Team members need varying amounts of **motivation and direction** -- just like plants need different amounts of light and water.
- Some are in a rut (need motivation), some are distracted (need direction), some are adrift (need both)
- Don't over-water -- giving motivation/direction to someone who doesn't need it is annoying

## Other Tips
- **Delegate, but get your hands dirty** -- take on a grungy task to gain respect
- **Seek to replace yourself** -- hire people smarter than you; give them chances to lead
- **Know when to make waves** -- don't wait; rare problems work themselves out
- **Shield your team from chaos** -- the world outside your team is chaotic; your previous manager shielded you from it
- **Give air cover** -- share info but don't distract with unlikely organizational craziness
- **Let them know when they're doing well** -- positive feedback matters
- **"It's easy to say yes to something easy to undo"** -- more things are undoable than you think
