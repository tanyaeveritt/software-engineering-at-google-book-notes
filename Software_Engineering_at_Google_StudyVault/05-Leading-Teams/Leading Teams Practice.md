## Question 1 - Three Leadership Roles [recall]
> Describe the three leadership roles Google recognizes and how they differ.

> [!answer]- Show Answer
> 1. **Engineering Manager**: leader of people; responsible for performance, productivity, and happiness of every team member; balances business needs with individual needs
> 2. **Tech Lead (TL)**: leads technology efforts; responsible for technology decisions, architecture, priorities, velocity, and project management; usually also an individual contributor
> 3. **Tech Lead Manager (TLM)**: handles both people and technical needs; common on small/nascent teams; tricky role that requires learning to balance individual work, delegation, and people management
> Larger, well-established teams at Google typically have a **pair** -- one TL and one engineering manager -- because doing both well without burning out is very difficult.

## Question 2 - Servant Leadership [recall]
> What is "servant leadership" and what advice did Steve Vinter give about management?

> [!answer]- Show Answer
> **Servant leadership** means the most important thing a leader can do is to **serve their team**, like a butler tending to a household. It involves creating an atmosphere of humility, respect, and trust -- removing bureaucratic obstacles, helping achieve consensus, and getting hands dirty when needed.
> Steve Vinter's advice: **"Above all, resist the urge to manage."** The greatest urge of newly minted managers is to actively "manage" employees, which typically has disastrous consequences. The cure is servant leadership.

## Question 3 - Six Antipatterns [recall]
> Name all six management antipatterns from the chapter and briefly describe each.

> [!answer]- Show Answer
> 1. **Hire Pushovers**: hire people less smart/ambitious to avoid being challenged; team becomes dependent and can't function without you
> 2. **Ignore Low Performers**: hoping they'll improve or leave; they don't; high performers carry them and eventually leave
> 3. **Ignore Human Issues**: managers from technical backgrounds neglect the human element; empathy matters
> 4. **Be Everyone's Friend**: confusing friendship with soft leadership; people feel pressure to artificially reciprocate when you hold power over their career
> 5. **Compromise the Hiring Bar**: picking "best 5 from 50" regardless of quality; fastest way to build a mediocre team
> 6. **Treat Team Like Children**: micromanaging or disrespecting abilities; people act the way you treat them

## Question 4 - Peter Principle Mitigation [recall]
> What is the Peter Principle and how does Google mitigate it?

> [!answer]- Show Answer
> The **Peter Principle** states that "in a hierarchy every employee tends to rise to their level of incompetence." Google mitigates this by requiring that a person **perform the job above their current level** for a period of time (i.e., "exceeds expectations" at their current level) before being promoted. This ensures people have demonstrated capability at the next level before officially moving into it.

## Question 5 - Failure Culture [recall]
> How does the chapter say leaders should handle individual successes and failures differently?

> [!answer]- Show Answer
> - Praise individual successes **publicly** in front of the team
> - Give constructive criticism for individual failures **privately**
> "Public criticism of an individual is not only ineffective (it puts people on the defense), but rarely necessary, and most often is just mean or cruel." The rest of the team already knows when someone has failed -- there's no need to rub it in. Always apply humility, respect, and trust.

## Question 6 - Compliment Sandwich [recall]
> Why does the chapter advise against using the "compliment sandwich" for feedback?

> [!answer]- Show Answer
> The **compliment sandwich** (praise + criticism + praise) softens the blow, but most people will only hear the compliments. Example: "You're a solid team member... your code is convoluted... but you've got great potential and cool T-shirts." The person walks out thinking "Sweet! I've got cool T-shirts!" and misses the critical message. Instead, be **kind and empathetic** but deliver criticism **directly and clearly**, without candy-coating. The Tim story shows that clear, direct feedback (without being mean) dramatically improved performance within weeks.

## Question 7 - Low Performer Coaching [application]
> One of your team members is consistently missing deadlines and producing below-average work. Other team members are starting to complain. Using the chapter's framework, how would you handle this?

> [!answer]- Show Answer
> Don't ignore it -- **"Hope is not a strategy"** (Google SRE motto). Act quickly:
> 1. Set up a **specific time frame** (e.g., two months) with very **specific, small, incremental, measurable goals**
> 2. Meet **weekly** to check progress with explicit expectations around each milestone
> 3. Apply **temporary micromanagement** with humility, respect, and trust -- especially respect
> 4. Think of it as helping a limping person learn to walk, then jog, then run alongside the team
> 5. If they can't keep up, it becomes obvious to both of you early -- they may choose to quit, or determination kicks in and they improve
>
> **Why act fast**: High performers waste time carrying the low performer, team morale leaks away, high performers leave, you eventually end up with a team of only low performers. Also, you aren't doing the low performer any favors -- they might have great impact somewhere else.

## Question 8 - New Manager Transition [application]
> You've just been promoted from individual contributor to tech lead of your own team. You notice you're spending all day in meetings and feel like you "didn't do anything." How should you reframe your thinking?

> [!answer]- Show Answer
> The chapter uses the **apples and bananas** metaphor. As an IC, you counted "apples" (code written, bugs closed, designs completed). As a manager, you're now growing "bananas" (enabling team happiness and productivity). Saying "I didn't pick any apples" while ignoring flourishing banana trees is the wrong measure.
> **Reframing**:
> - Your output is now measured by your team's output, not your personal code
> - Making it possible for your team to be happy and productive **is** your job
> - Management work pays off over a **longer timeline** -- don't expect immediate visible artifacts
> - Consider both "reasons to lead": it's a way to **scale yourself** (imagine how much code a whole team writes under your leadership), and you might actually be good at it
> - Follow Steve Vinter's advice: "resist the urge to manage" -- serve the team instead

## Question 9 - Team Direction Scenario [application]
> Your team of 8 engineers is working on a product, but progress feels slow and scattered. Different members seem to be pulling in different directions. What positive pattern from the chapter addresses this?

> [!answer]- Show Answer
> This calls for **Set Clear Goals**. The chapter uses the metaphor of a big truck with ropes: each team member pulls in their own direction. Without alignment, they waste energy pulling every which way instead of all pulling north.
> **Actions**:
> 1. Create a **concise mission statement** for the team
> 2. Set clear **priorities** and help the team decide how to make trade-offs
> 3. Once direction and goals are defined, **step back and give autonomy**, periodically checking in
> 4. This frees up your time for other leadership tasks and drastically increases team efficiency
> Without clear goals, teams can still succeed but "waste a great deal of energy" in scattered effort.

## Question 10 - Zen Master Leadership [analysis]
> The chapter describes a "gears" analogy and says "the leader is always on stage." Analyze what this means for how leaders should conduct themselves day-to-day.

> [!answer]- Show Answer
> The **gears analogy**: The org chart is a chain of gears. An individual contributor is a tiny gear; each successive manager is a larger gear. When the CEO's large gear makes a small movement, it sends the IC's tiny gear spinning wildly through the chain. This means leaders' actions are **amplified** through the organization -- even small reactions cascade.
> **"Always on stage"** means: you're watched not just in meetings or talks, but sitting at your desk answering emails, eating lunch, having small talk. Peers read your body language for confidence or fear.
> **Day-to-day implications**:
> - **Mediate your reactions** -- skepticism and cynicism are liabilities in leaders
> - **Maintain calm** like Bill Coughran -- his calm demeanor helped panicked engineers focus on solutions instead of panic
> - **Ask questions** instead of jumping to solutions -- help people solve problems themselves (Socratic method)
> - Your visible attitude about **everything, no matter how trivial** -- spreads infectiously to your team
> This is why emotional regulation becomes more important the higher you go.

## Question 11 - Influence Without Authority [analysis]
> The chapter describes the Data Liberation Front as a case study in "influence without authority." Analyze the strategies they used and how they apply to leadership more broadly.

> [!answer]- Show Answer
> The **Data Liberation Front** had fewer than 6 engineers but got 50+ Google products to export data through Google Takeout, with no executive directive. Their strategies:
> 1. **Identified a strategic need** for the company (data portability)
> 2. **Linked it to existing mission and priorities** -- framed it as aligned with what Google already valued
> 3. **Built a tool** that made it quick and easy for teams to integrate -- removed the barrier to participation
> 4. **Didn't demand compliance** -- made participation attractive and low-friction
>
> **Broader leadership lessons**:
> - You can lead without formal authority by **building consensus** (a skill often used by unofficial leaders)
> - Directing and dictating is less effective than consensus-building, even when you have authority
> - The pattern applies beyond management: **any engineer** can influence the organization by identifying needs, building solutions, and making adoption easy
> - Jeff Dean is another example -- led only a fraction of engineering but influenced the entire organization through expertise, writing, and speaking
